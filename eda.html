EDA, linear regression, and predictive modeling on population groups by shelter-cost-to-income ratio and core housing need.¶

Zeliha Ural Merpez September 4, 2025

This project explores population groups in Ontario’s census subdivisions using Statistics Canada's dataset on shelter-cost-to-income ratios and core housing need. The goal is to apply exploratory data analysis, regression modeling, and predictive techniques to understand how housing affordability impacts different demographic groups.

By analyzing this dataset, we aim to uncover patterns and relationships between income levels, shelter costs, and housing adequacy—providing insights into the factors contributing to core housing need across Ontario.

Summary

The analysis includes four key steps: exploratory data analysis, data preprocessing and feature engineering, fitting and evaluating a Poisson regression model, and interpreting results through detailed model summaries and visualizations.

Steps:

  • Exploratory Data Analysis
  • Feature Engineering
  • Poisson Regression Modeling & Summary Interpretation
  • Results Visualization

Interpreting the Histograms¶

The figure above shows the distribution of percentages for:

  • In Core Housing Need
  • Not in Core Housing Need
  • Affordable Housing
  • Unaffordable Housing

Each variable has been normalized within its respective subgroup and expressed as a percentage (0–100%).

Key Observations¶

  • Distributions are not normal: None of the four indicators follow a normal distribution. This is evident from the sharp peaks near 0 or 100 and the long tails in the histograms.

  • Heavy clustering at 0%:

    • All four distributions show a high number of observations at 0%.
    • This occurs because many census subdivisions report no data for certain population groups (e.g., smaller visible minority groups, specific age brackets), resulting in a valid percentage of zero.

  • Right-skewed distributions:

    • In Core Need and Unaffordable Housing are strongly right-skewed, with most values near 0% and a few high outliers.
    • This indicates that only a small number of subdivisions have high percentages of households in need or living in unaffordable housing.

  • Left-skewed or bimodal distributions:

    • Not in Core Need and Affordable Housing have more dispersed values, with many subdivisions near 100%, and others at or near 0%.
    • This leads to left-skewed or even bimodal shapes, reflecting stark differences in housing adequacy across regions.

What This Tells Us¶

  • The skewed and non-normal distributions emphasize the inequality in housing conditions across Ontario’s census subdivisions.
  • Some areas show almost universal access to affordable housing or lack of core housing need, while others reflect acute challenges.
  • These patterns are shaped by demographics, geography, and housing stock availability, and must be considered in modeling or policy targeting.

The correlation heatmap reveals interesting relationships among the four housing indicators. There is a strong positive correlation (0.96) between not in core need and affordable housing, suggesting that areas with more affordable housing tend to have fewer households in core housing need. Conversely, in core need shows a high positive correlation (0.73) with unaffordable housing, indicating that higher unaffordability is associated with greater housing need. The weaker correlations between other pairs highlight distinct dynamics between affordability and housing need categories across different regions. These insights can guide targeted housing policy and resource allocation.

# Notes on Interpretation:
- Coefficients represent the **effect relative to the baseline category** for each categorical variable.
- For example:
  - `gender_Women+` coefficient shows the effect of being Women **compared to the baseline gender** (likely Men or other).
  - `tenure_Owner`, `tenure_Renter`, etc. are effects relative to the base tenure category (e.g., "No tenure" or whichever category is excluded).
  - `SSM_` prefixed features represent effects of different Service Management areas relative to the baseline region (the omitted category).
- The `% Change` column shows how much the odds/risk increase (↑) or decrease (↓) relative to the base category.
- The `Significant` column marks whether the coefficient is statistically significant (p-value < 0.05).

This summary shows how different characteristics affect the likelihood of being in core housing need, based on a statistical model.

All comparisons below are relative to a person who is:

  • Male
  • Lives in non-subsidized housing
  • Primary household maintainer
  • Aged 0–17 years
  • Located in Algoma

💡 Interpretation Guide¶

  • Positive % (↑) → More likely to be in core housing need compared to base person.
  • Negative % (↓) → Less likely to be in need.
  • Exponentiated Coefficient (exp(coef)) translates raw model output into understandable % changes.
  • All values shown are statistically significant (p < 0.05).

Example Scenario¶

A woman aged 65+ living in Toronto in subsidized housing is more than 2,400% more likely to be in core housing need than a 17-year-old male, primary household maintainer living in Algoma.
